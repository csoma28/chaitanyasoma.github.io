Denarius Management: A $1B Factor-Tilted Portfolio

University of Bath • MN30470 Investment and Trading

The Mandate

A 50-year-old client, an 8% target, an inheritance plan

The brief: invest $1 billion on behalf of a 50-year-old business owner planning to transfer the wealth to his sons. The fund had to deliver an 8% annualised return over a 10-year horizon to beat inflation and grow the principal, while staying risk-averse and ESG-aligned. Charging a competitive 60 bps annual management fee, Denarius positioned itself as a diversified active manager.

My Role

Chief Risk Officer

As CRO, I was accountable for the portfolio's risk profile: defining the factor tilts that controlled exposure, monitoring the political, industry, and liquidity risks across positions, and ensuring our diversification choices held up against the stress tests we ran each quarter. I also owned the risk-and-return reporting that compared our fund against the strategy and policy benchmarks.

Strategy

Factor-based, ESG-screened, four-stage execution

  • Stage 1 — ESG screen. Removed all equities with an ESG score below 67 (with one explicit exception for Tesla on engagement-rationale grounds), and excluded names with a market cap below $10 billion to control liquidity risk.
  • Stage 2 — Factor tilts. Selected six factors aligned with the client's mandate — Value, Size, Dividend Yield, Low-Leverage, ESG, and Low-Volatility — and assigned tilt weights that prioritised long-term capital growth over yield.
  • Stage 3 — Reweighting. Combined the tilt weights into a final equity allocation, with developed equities accounting for 53.58% of the portfolio.
  • Stage 4 — Quarterly rebalancing. Re-ran the model quarterly to reset weights, manage drift, and respond to macro shocks.

Factor Tilt Logic

Where the weights came from

  • Size (0.90). Heaviest tilt — smaller companies have historically delivered higher risk-adjusted returns, aligning with our "Finding Value in the Future" investment belief.
  • Value (0.50) and ESG (0.50). Equal weight, balancing return generation with the client's responsible-investing mandate.
  • Dividend Yield (0.30) and Low-Leverage (0.30). Moderate tilts; dividends can be smoothed by management, and some leverage is healthy for growth.
  • Low-Volatility (0.25). Lightest tilt — useful for risk control but weak as a return thesis on its own.

The fund showed measurably lower volatility than both the benchmark and the S&P 500 during the late-November risk-off window — particularly between 19/10 – 24/10 and 22/11 – 28/11/22. The downside: a Sharpe ratio of -2.1 over the short window, reflecting the cash drag and the difficulty of generating excess return in a 16-day high-vol regime.

Reflections

What 16 days teaches you about a 10-year strategy

The trading window was less than 1% of our intended horizon, which made any backward-looking metric (VaR, Sharpe, max drawdown) more theatre than truth. The real value of the engagement was the discipline of building and defending an investment policy, the factor selection logic, and the risk reporting framework — the same scaffolding any real fund would rely on.

"In hindsight, we could have benefitted from strategically timing our investments to ensure that we invested into different markets on the same day. The challenge taught us the value of attention to detail — a minor overlook in our initial factor tilts analysis cost us materially in transaction fees to fix."
