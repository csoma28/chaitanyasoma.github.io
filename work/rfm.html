Customer RFM Segmentation with KMeans

UK Online Retail II Dataset • 525,461 transactions

A clustering project that turns a year of raw e-commerce transactions into actionable customer segments. By engineering Recency, Frequency, and Monetary value features and feeding them through KMeans, the analysis surfaces who the high-value customers are, who's drifting, and where retention spend should land.

Dataset
Online Retail II (UCI)
Volume
525K rows / 4,383 customers
Method
RFM + KMeans
Stack

The Question

Who actually drives the revenue?

For most retailers, a small share of customers generates the majority of revenue — but identifying them requires more than a simple sales rank. RFM analysis adds two missing dimensions: how recently a customer purchased, and how often they come back. Together, these three signals describe loyalty, value, and risk in a way that flat sales data can't.

Approach

From raw invoices to clean segments

  • Data cleaning. Stripped cancelled invoices (those starting with "C"), bad-debt adjustments ("A" prefixes), null Customer IDs, and stock codes that didn't match the standard 5-digit pattern. About 20% of the rows were dropped to get a defensible analytical base.
  • Feature engineering. Built three customer-level features: Recency (days since last purchase relative to a fixed reference date), Frequency (count of distinct invoices), and Monetary (sum of Quantity × Price across the year).
  • Scaling. Applied StandardScaler so the magnitude difference between Frequency (single digits) and Monetary (thousands of pounds) wouldn't dominate the distance metric.
  • Cluster selection. Used the elbow method on within-cluster sum of squares and silhouette scores to pick a stable number of clusters.
  • Profiling. Labelled each cluster with a business-friendly name (Champions, Loyal, At-Risk, Hibernating) by reading mean RFM values.
What the Segments Reveal

Four behavioural archetypes

Champions
Recent, frequent, high-spend. Reward, retain, refer. The smallest group with the largest revenue share.
Loyal
Frequent buyers with moderate basket size. Upsell candidates and brand advocates.
At-Risk
High historical value but Recency drifting. Win-back campaigns earn back the most revenue here.
Hibernating
Low across all three. Cheap to ignore, expensive to chase — deprioritise.
Why RFM

Three numbers that explain a thousand customers

RFM survives as a workhorse for a reason: it's interpretable, computationally cheap, and maps directly to marketing levers. Pairing it with KMeans turns the manual quantile cuts into data-driven groupings, and the silhouette scoring keeps the analyst honest about whether the clusters are real.

The lesson from this project wasn't that KMeans found magic groupings — it was that defensible features beat fancy models. RFM done well outperforms a neural network done lazily.
